What it answers

The "almonds versus data centers" water debate, and the broader fight over who gets scarce water and power, usually trades in numbers nobody can picture — millions of gallons, hundreds of megawatts. The Resource Ledger converts those into something legible by holding every large user to the same measure:

  • What does a 100-megawatt data center draw, in households of electricity or acres of almonds of water?
  • How does an almond orchard compare to an alfalfa field — or to a golf course — acre for acre?
  • How much water does a semiconductor fab use against a small city?
  • Why does a power plant's water withdrawal look enormous while its consumption is tiny?

How the comparison works

Each user carries a resource intensity — water and electricity per acre, per megawatt, per facility, per household, per year. To compare two users, the ledger converts your chosen quantity into base units (acre-feet per year for water, MWh per year for power), then expresses it as the equivalent number of any other user. Three things are baked in:

  • Three separate tracks. Water withdrawn, water consumed, and electricity are never cross-converted — you can only compare like with like.
  • Ranges, not points. Every figure is a low / typical / high estimate, and the range carries through every comparison.
  • Operational only. It counts the running draw, not the water and energy embodied in construction or equipment.

Withdrawal is not consumption. A once-through-cooled power plant withdraws 20,000–50,000 gallons of water per megawatt-hour but consumes only a few hundred — almost all of it returns to the river. An almond orchard consumes nearly everything it is given. Conflating the two is the most common mistake in water debates, so the ledger keeps them on separate tracks and lets you switch between them.

Why symmetry is the point

Data centers are the timely reason to look, but they are not the target. Every user — an orchard, a city, a chip fab, a data center — is measured exactly the same way and shows up as a row in the same ledger. That symmetry is deliberate: it makes this an accounting of what large users draw, not a verdict on any one of them. A developer and a conservation group should both be able to cite it and simply disagree about which row matters.

Frequently asked questions

What does the Resource Ledger compare?

It puts large users of water and electricity on a single scale — almonds, alfalfa, cities, power plants, semiconductor fabs, golf courses, and data centers — and expresses what any one of them draws in terms of the others. Pick a user and a quantity, choose a resource (water consumed, water withdrawn, or electricity), and it returns the equivalent number of households, acres, power plants, and so on.

What is the difference between water withdrawal and consumption?

Withdrawal is water drawn from a source; consumption is the portion that is not returned — evaporated, transpired, or otherwise lost. They can differ enormously: a once-through-cooled power plant withdraws 20,000–50,000 gallons per MWh but consumes only a few hundred, because most of the water returns to the river. The Resource Ledger keeps the two on separate tracks and never mixes them.

Isn't this just an argument against data centers?

No. Every user is measured exactly the same way — an almond orchard and a data center are just rows in the same ledger. The symmetry is the point: it is an accounting of what large users draw, not a verdict on any one of them. Data centers are a timely example, not the target.

Where does the data come from?

Every figure is public and cited inline: EPA WaterSense (household water), EIA (residential electricity), UC ANR, the Pacific Institute, and UC Davis (crop water), the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory 2024 data-center report (data-center energy and water), the Union of Concerned Scientists and NREL (power-plant cooling), the World Economic Forum and Manufacturing Dive (semiconductor fabs), and the GCSAA (golf courses).

Why is every number shown as a range?

Real-world intensities vary. Data-center cooling water spans roughly five-fold depending on the cooling system; crop water depends on climate and irrigation. Every figure carries a low, typical, and high estimate, and the range propagates through every comparison. A range is the honest answer; a single number would imply a precision the underlying data does not have.

Is the Resource Ledger free?

Yes. It is free to use with no signup, every input is public, and the full dataset, sources, and methodology are open.
